Main Takeaway

Whole Foods has consistently higher average prices in all food processing levels, even in highly processed foods, while Walmart is the cheapest and Target is somewhere in the middle. Whole Foods shoppers realize that they are paying a premium for food at Whole Foods. In return, the expectation is that the food they purchase is much healthier and less processed. However, I think it would surprise people to see that Whole Foods is charging their customers noticeably more than Target and Walmart even for the unhealthy foods that they stock. In summary, Whole Foods is charging its shoppers extra no matter the food processing category, when the expectation would be that shoppers are only paying a premium for their healthier options.

Visualization Design Decisions

To best represent this takeaway, I chose to group the data by the Food Processing Class (FPro_class) and Store (store). I then calculated the average for each store at each processing level. I chose a barplot to represent this because it allows for easy visual comparisons between the average prices of the stores and processing levels.

  • Grouped Bars by Processing Level: Bars are grouped by processing class so that within one class, viewers can compare each store directly.
  • Consistent Store Colors: Each store uses its brand color (blue for Walmart, red for Target, green for Whole Foods) to help viewers quickly identify stores across categories.
  • Gridlines Added: Light gridlines improve perspective and make comparisons of bar heights easier for viewers.
  • Uniform Axis Scale: Bars start at the same baseline and use a standard scale because no bars dominate the range, preserving visibility of all differences.
  • Ordered Bars: Within each processing class, bars are ordered Walmart, Target, Whole Foods to highlight the consistent price increase across stores.

Obscured Data Acknowledgment

Using mean prices obscures the underlying distribution of data. Readers cannot see how many items contribute to each average or the variance in prices (e.g., whether the ultra-processed category at Walmart is based on 3,000 items or just 30). This trade-off simplifies comparison at the cost of distributional insight.
